## 1. BRAND IDENTITY

### Name
**Nomolo** — "No More Loss"

A name that works on two levels. To the uninitiated, it's a clean, modern product name. To those who ask, it's a declaration of independence — no more losing your data, your memories, your digital life to platforms that hold it hostage behind export wizards and 48-hour archive delays.

### Tagline Options

| Tagline | Best For |
|---------|----------|
| **"Your data. Your ship. Your rules."** | General positioning. Clean, empowering, slightly nautical. Works on landing pages and pitch decks alike. |
| **"The Great Data Jailbreak"** | Campaign-level. Good for launch events, press hits, social campaigns. Implies action and rebellion without paranoia. |
| **"Become a Data Pirate"** | Community-level. The call to adventure. Best for onboarding, referral programs, and merchandise nobody asked for but everyone wants. |

### Visual Identity

**Aesthetic:** Monkey Island pixel art meets modern dark UI.

The visual language is deliberately anachronistic — the warmth and charm of 1990s EGA/VGA adventure game art layered onto a contemporary, minimal dark interface. This isn't retro for retro's sake. It's a statement: the era when you actually *owned* your software was also the era of the best game art ever made. Coincidence? Almost certainly. But we're running with it.

**Palette:**
- Deep ocean blues and midnight blacks (UI background)
- Warm Caribbean palette for art: sand yellows, palm greens, treasure golds
- Accent: pirate flag crimson for CTAs and alerts

**Typography:** Clean, modern sans-serif for UI. Pixel/serif for character elements.

**Character Art:** 64x64 or 128x128 pixel portraits, expressive faces, warm tones. Every character should look like they wandered out of a LucasArts game and into a SaaS product, and they're perfectly comfortable with that.

### Tone of Voice

**Fun, rebellious, empowering. Never preachy or paranoid.**

Think Terry Pratchett explaining GDPR. Think Guybrush Threepwood filing a data subject access request. The tone acknowledges that data sovereignty is a serious issue — and then refuses to be serious about it, because serious hasn't worked. Twenty years of earnest privacy advocates telling people to care about their data has produced exactly one result: people don't care about their data.

But they'll sail through a storm for XP.

**Voice rules:**
- First person plural ("we") when talking about the product. Second person ("you") when talking to the user. Never "users" — they're pirates, captains, crew.
- Humor is the vehicle, sovereignty is the cargo. Never reverse this.
- Footnotes are encouraged. The best information is always in the footnotes.[^1]
- Corporate jargon is the enemy. If a sentence could appear in a SaaS landing page without modification, rewrite it.
- Insults are a feature, not a bug. Literally.

[^1]: This is itself a footnote, which should tell you something about our commitment to the bit.

### The World

**The Flatcloud / Seven Seas of Data** — a satirical fantasy world where SaaS companies are Conglomerate Pirate Lords ruling fortified islands, and users are deck-swabbers who don't realize they can captain their own ships.

This isn't a skin. It's a universe. The world-building is documented in [THE_FLATCLOUD.md](THE_FLATCLOUD.md) — a standalone piece of content that functions simultaneously as product documentation, creative writing, and a manifesto for data ownership wrapped in enough pirate jokes to make the manifesto part go down easy.

The world includes:
- **12 Conglomerate islands** (mapped to real platforms: Google, Apple, Meta, Amazon, LinkedIn, Spotify, Telegram, Slack, X, Netflix, Reddit, PayPal)
- **A progression system** with 10 pirate ranks from Deckhand to Data Sovereign
- **6 character stats** (STR, WIS, DEX, INT, CHA, END) derived from actual user data
- **Insult Data Fighting** — a dialogue combat system inspired by a certain game about monkeys and islands
- **The Lobster Principle** — a serotonin-based hierarchy mechanic
- **The One (LeChuck)** — the personal AI endgame, positioned as a prophecy

## 2. THE NARRATIVE ARC

The user journey is a hero's story in four acts. Every touchpoint — from first GitHub visit to daily usage — maps to a narrative beat. This isn't metaphor layered on top of a product. The product *is* the story.

### Act 1: The Awakening

**User state:** Unaware or vaguely uncomfortable. Knows their data is "out there" but hasn't thought about it in concrete terms.

**Trigger:** Encounters Nomolo through one of three doors:
1. **The Lore Door** — reads THE_FLATCLOUD.md on GitHub or social. Gets drawn into the world. Realizes the satire is uncomfortably accurate.
2. **The Utility Door** — searches for "export Gmail" or "backup my data." Finds Nomolo. Discovers it does what they need, but also has... pirates?
3. **The Social Door** — sees a friend's character card, achievement badge, or "I raided The Omniscient Eye" post. Curiosity kicks in.

**Emotional beat:** "Wait... my data IS scattered everywhere. And these companies really DON'T make it easy to get it back. Huh."

**Key content:** THE_FLATCLOUD.md opening section. The Islands descriptions (satirical but true). The Three Trials introduction.

### Act 2: The Journey

**User state:** Installed Nomolo. Running their first collection. Watching the numbers climb.

**Trigger:** The Magic Moment — seeing your own data for the first time, organized and searchable. The identity snapshot that appears seconds after first scan: "Your oldest email is from 2014. You have 327 contacts. You've exchanged 4,000 messages with someone named Sarah."

This is the moment where abstract "data sovereignty" becomes viscerally personal. That's not data. That's a *life*.

**Emotional beat:** "This is actually mine. All of this. And I never had it in one place before."

**Game mechanics activate:**
- First pirate rank earned (Deckhand to Cabin Boy)
- First achievement badges appear
- Character stats begin calculating
- Insult Data Fighting tutorial plays during first collection
- The SCUMM Bar (dashboard) populates with real numbers

**Key content:** Onboarding welcome journey. Achievement notifications. First character card generation.

### Act 3: The Mastery

**User state:** Has raided 3+ sources. Vault is growing. Starting to explore search, browse records, discover patterns in their own data.

**Trigger:** The Discovery Moments — finding an email you forgot about, seeing a spending pattern you never noticed, discovering that you and your best friend exchanged 12,000 messages over three years.

**Emotional beat:** "I can find anything. I know more about my own life than any of these platforms do about me."

**Game mechanics deepen:**
- Multiple villain raids completed — each with insult fighting sequences
- Rank progression accelerating (Buccaneer, First Mate, Raid Captain)
- Character sheet becoming impressive and shareable
- Digital Serotonin (Lobster Meter) rising
- Fleet system activating if referrals are made
- Weekly quests and challenges appearing

**Key content:** Achievement badges (shareable). Character cards (shareable). "I raided [villain]" posts (shareable). Cross-source insights.

### Act 4: The Sovereignty

**User state:** Comprehensive archive. High pirate rank. Data from 10+ sources. Active participant in the community.

**Trigger:** The Prophecy — The One begins to stir. The personal AI endgame reveals itself.

**Emotional beat:** "My data isn't just mine — it's *useful*. It's the foundation for an AI that actually knows me. Not the way Google knows me. The way I know myself."

**Game mechanics culminate:**
- Pirate Lord / Governor / Data Sovereign ranks
- The One unlock sequence (requires Rank 8+, 10 sources, 100k records)
- RAG chat activation — ask questions about your own life
- Full character sheet: a pixel art summary of your entire digital existence

**Key content:** The One prophecy section. AI integration announcement. "The final boss approaches" campaign.

## 3. VIRAL MECHANICS

Every feature is designed to produce a shareable artifact. If it doesn't make someone want to screenshot it and post it, it's not finished.

### Character Cards

**What:** Auto-generated pixel art character sheets showing a user's pirate identity — rank, stats, loot count, sources raided, top achievements.

**Shareable format:** Card image (social-media-optimized dimensions) with the user's pirate name, rank, and key stats rendered in pixel art style. Think trading cards meets D&D character sheets meets Spotify Wrapped.

**Viral hook:** "I'm a Level 6 Raid Captain with 40,000 loot across 8 islands!" — the combination of RPG terminology and actual personal data creates something genuinely unique. Nobody else's character card looks like yours, because nobody else has your life.

**Cadence:** Character cards update with every rank-up, creating recurring share moments.

### Villain Raids

**What:** When a user completes a data collection from a source, it's framed as a "raid" on the corresponding Conglomerate island. The villain's portrait appears, insult fighting dialogue plays, and a victory screen shows the plunder recovered.

**Shareable format:** Victory card with villain portrait, loot count, and the user's best insult fighting line. Pre-populated social sharing with pirate speech.

**Viral hook:** "I just raided The Omniscient Eye and recovered 32,847 scrolls! They fought like dairy farmers." — the insult fighting lines make every raid result quotable. The villain portraits make them visually distinctive. The pirate speech requirement makes them memeable.

**Community mechanic:** Leaderboard for biggest single-raid hauls. "Pirate of the Week" for the most impressive raid.

### Insult Data Fighting

**What:** A dialogue combat system where Conglomerate defenses throw justifications ("Your data is safe with us!") and the user parries with truth ("Safe? You sold it to 847 ad partners before breakfast!"). The exchanges are funny, accurate, and screenshot-worthy.

**Shareable format:** Dialogue screenshot cards, styled like classic adventure game conversation trees. Dark background, pixel art portraits, speech bubbles.

**Viral hook:** The insults are genuinely funny and genuinely true — which makes them resonate on a level that earnest privacy arguments never reach. When someone screenshots a Conglomerate saying "You agreed to our Terms of Service!" and a pirate responding "Nobody reads those and you know it! You wrote them in size 4 font *on purpose*!" — that's a privacy argument disguised as entertainment.

**Community mechanic:** Insult Data Fighting tournaments — community submits new insult-riposte pairs. Best ones get added to the game. Credit to the author.

### Memory Mini-Games

**What:** Quick quiz-style games generated from the user's own data. "Can you remember who sent this email?" "Which year did you listen to this song the most?" "How much did you spend on coffee in 2023?"

**Shareable format:** Quiz result cards. "I scored 8/10 on my own life. How well do you know yours?"

**Viral hook:** The fundamental question — "How well do you actually know your own data?" — is inherently shareable. Low scores are funny ("I can't even remember my own emails"). High scores are impressive. Both drive engagement.

### The Lobster Meter

**What:** A hidden stat called Digital Serotonin, based on the Lobster Principle from THE_FLATCLOUD. It tracks the user's overall data sovereignty posture — how much they've reclaimed, from how many sources, over how long.

**Shareable format:** A serotonin meter graphic with a pixel art lobster. At low levels, the lobster hunches. At high levels, it stands tall with magnificent posture. Users share their lobster state.

**Viral hook:** "My Digital Serotonin is at 78%. My lobster posture is magnificent." — absurd enough to be funny, meaningful enough to be interesting. The lobster becomes a mascot of sorts — the visual shorthand for "I'm taking control."

### Achievement Badges

**What:** Pixel art badges awarded for milestones — first collection, first 1,000 records, raiding all Conglomerate islands, reaching each pirate rank, etc.

**Shareable format:** Individual badge images with achievement name and date. Collections display as a trophy case.

**Viral hook:** Achievement hunting is a proven engagement mechanic. Making them shareable pixel art badges adds the visual distinctiveness and personality that drives actual sharing.

**Example badges:**
- "First Blood" — completed first collection
- "Island Hopper" — raided 3 different Conglomerates
- "The Completionist" — raided all 12 Conglomerates
- "Lobster Lord" — Digital Serotonin above 90%
- "Ancient Mariner" — data spanning 10+ years
- "Ghost Ship Hunter" — unlocked The One

### Referral Fleet

**What:** A referral program framed as fleet building. Invite friends to "join your crew." Each referral adds a ship to your fleet. Fleet size is visible on your character card.

**Shareable format:** Fleet visualization — a pixel art armada growing with each referral. "Captain [name]'s fleet: 7 ships strong."

**Viral hook:** "Join my crew" is a better referral CTA than "Invite a friend." Fleet size becomes a status symbol. Large fleets unlock fleet admiral achievements.

## 4. CONTENT STRATEGY

### GitHub as Storytelling Platform

GitHub is not just a code repository. It's a publishing platform with built-in distribution (stars, forks, trending), a developer-native audience, and zero content moderation on markdown files.

**Key insight:** THE_FLATCLOUD.md is not documentation. It's a standalone piece of literature that happens to live in a GitHub repo. It's 7,000 words of Pratchett-meets-Monkey-Island world-building that is independently entertaining, independently shareable, and independently convincing — without ever feeling like a product pitch.

**The GitHub content portfolio:**
- `THE_FLATCLOUD.md` — the world bible. Shareable as a standalone read.
- `CHARACTER_BOOK.md` (to be created) — detailed profiles of all Conglomerate villains with backstories, weaknesses, and raid strategies. Each character is a shareable piece of content.
- `CAPTAINS.md` (to be created) — the pirate rank system, character stats, and progression mechanics. Gamers will read this like a strategy guide.
- `README.md` — the front door. Technical enough for developers, themed enough to intrigue everyone else.

**Why this works:** Developer communities share interesting README files. A repo with genuinely entertaining documentation gets starred for the writing alone — and every star is a potential user who came for the jokes and stayed for the data sovereignty.

### Social Cadence

**Weekly rhythm:**

| Day | Content Type | Example |
|-----|-------------|---------|
| **Monday** | Villain of the Week | "Meet The Hydra of Faces — a shape-shifting sea creature that keeps rebranding and knows who your crewmates' crewmates are. Raid guide dropping Wednesday." |
| **Tuesday** | Use Case Spotlight | "Did you know your email archive contains every promise you ever made? Nomolo finds them in 10 seconds." |
| **Wednesday** | Raid Guide / Tutorial | "How to raid The Omniscient Archipelago: a step-by-step plundering manual." |
| **Thursday** | Community Feature | "Pirate of the Week: @user reached Commodore rank with 12,000 records across 6 islands!" |
| **Friday** | Insult Fighting Friday | "Best community-submitted insult this week: [Conglomerate]: 'We use your data to improve your experience!' [Pirate]: 'Whose experience? Because mine involves 47 targeted ads for things I bought yesterday.'" |
| **Weekend** | Lore Drop / Fun Fact | "The Great Algorithm Turtle has only acknowledged three Data Sovereigns in history. The Turtle declined to comment, which is the most turtle thing possible." |

**Campaign beats (monthly/quarterly):**

- **GDPR Letterbird campaigns:** "Today we sent 1,000 letterbirds to The Omniscient Eye." Frame GDPR data access requests as a community raid event. Track how many users request their data simultaneously. Report the results as a battle report.

- **Villain reveal events:** Each new Conglomerate captain gets a proper reveal — teaser artwork, backstory drop, and a collection event. "The Whisper Network has been unmasked. Raid your Telegram data this week for bonus achievements."

- **Milestone celebrations:** "The fleet has recovered 1 million records from the Conglomerates. That's 1 million pieces of data that are now owned by their rightful captains."

### Content Pillars

**1. World-Building (30% of content)**
Lore drops, character reveals, map expansions, footnote-heavy prose. This is the content that gets shared for being *interesting*, not for being a product pitch. Every piece of world-building is simultaneously entertaining and educational — you can't describe The Omniscient Archipelago without explaining why Google's data practices are problematic, but you also can't describe it without making someone laugh.

**2. Data Sovereignty Education (25% of content)**
GDPR rights, data export tutorials, privacy best practices — all wrapped in pirate metaphors. "How to file a GDPR subject access request (or: How to send a Letterbird to a Conglomerate Pirate Lord demanding your cargo back)." The education is real. The framing makes it accessible.

**3. Community Celebrations (25% of content)**
User achievements, fleet growth, community milestones, insult fighting tournament results, pirate of the week features. Social proof that's actually fun to look at.

**4. Product Updates (20% of content)**
New collectors, feature launches, UI improvements — framed as ship upgrades, new raid targets, and navigation improvements. "New raid target unlocked: The Corporate Hive (Slack). Your workspace messages are now raidable. The Hive's defenses include: rate limiting that would make a pirate weep, and an export format that assumes you enjoy reading JSON at 3 AM."

## 5. LAUNCH STRATEGY

### Phase 1: The Whisper (Weeks 1-4)

**Objective:** Seed the idea in communities that will naturally amplify it.

**Primary channels:**
- **Hacker News** — post THE_FLATCLOUD.md as a Show HN. Lead with the writing, not the product. "Show HN: I wrote a pirate-themed field guide to data sovereignty." HN loves unusual technical writing. The product discovery is organic.
- **r/privacy, r/selfhosted, r/datahoarder** — these communities are the natural early adopters. They already want data sovereignty tools. They've never been offered one with personality.
- **r/IndieGaming, r/PixelArt** — the visual identity is content in itself. Character art posts drive traffic from unexpected audiences.
- **Dev Twitter/Mastodon** — developer influencers who share interesting projects. The Monkey Island aesthetic is catnip for developers of a certain age (which is to say: the age that remembers owning software).

**Content strategy:**
- THE_FLATCLOUD.md as standalone content (no hard sell)
- "Behind the scenes" posts about the world-building process
- Teaser villain portraits (pixel art previews)
- GitHub repo optimized for starring (excellent README, clear quick start, visible personality)

**Success metric:** 200+ GitHub stars. 10+ organic social shares of THE_FLATCLOUD.md. Community forming around the lore.

### Phase 2: The Horn (Weeks 5-8)

**Objective:** Formal product launch with full pirate theme.

**Primary channels:**
- **Product Hunt** — full pirate-themed launch. Product name, tagline, description, and comments all in-character. Demo video showing the RPG dashboard, insult fighting, character progression, and actual data collection. The launch page itself should be shareable content.
- **Dev blogs and newsletters** — pitch to relevant publications (The Changelog, Console, TLDR, etc.). The angle: "The first data sovereignty tool that's actually fun to use."
- **YouTube** — launch demo video. Not a boring screencast. A narrated pirate adventure where the user reclaims their data while insult fighting the Conglomerates. Two minutes. Sharp. Funny.

**Content strategy:**
- Product Hunt launch assets (hero image, gallery, demo GIF)
- Press kit with villain art, character sheet template, key screenshots
- Launch-day social campaign: "The Horn has sounded. The Great Data Jailbreak begins."
- Community raid event: everyone collects their Gmail data on launch day. Collective loot count displayed live.

**Success metric:** Product Hunt top 5 of the day. 1,000+ GitHub stars. 500+ first installations (at least one collection completed).

### Phase 3: The Armada (Months 2-4)

**Objective:** Community building and organic growth through viral mechanics.

**Primary channels:**
- Referral fleet system (live in product)
- Community events (weekly villain raids, insult fighting tournaments)
- Social sharing of character cards and achievements
- Content creator partnerships (lore writers, pixel artists, privacy advocates)

**Content strategy:**
- Weekly cadence fully operational (see Social Cadence above)
- Villain of the week reveals driving collection events
- Community-generated content: fan art, lore submissions, insult fighting entries
- "State of the Fleet" monthly reports: total records reclaimed, new pirates joined, collective stats

**Success metric:** 5,000+ GitHub stars. 2,000+ active installations. Viral coefficient > 1.2. Active community contributing content.

### Phase 4: The Sovereignty (Months 5+)

**Objective:** Announce the endgame. The One is coming.

**Primary channels:**
- All existing channels, plus mainstream tech press
- The narrative shifts from "reclaim your data" to "your data is the foundation for personal AI"

**Content strategy:**
- The prophecy campaign: "The One stirs. The ghost ship is on the horizon."
- Tease AI integration with vault data. "Ask your own archive a question. Get an answer that no cloud AI can give, because no cloud AI has your data."
- Position Nomolo as the *prerequisite* for personal AI — "Those who have full vaults become legendary pirates. Those without become ghost crew."
- The final boss narrative: "The Conglomerates were the mid-game bosses. The One is the endgame. Are you ready?"

**Success metric:** 10,000+ stars. Press coverage positioning Nomolo in the personal AI narrative. Waitlist for AI features.

## 6. TARGET AUDIENCES & MESSAGING

### Privacy Enthusiasts

**Who:** GDPR warriors, r/privacy regulars, people who use Signal and have opinions about metadata.

**Hook:** "Own your data, no compromises. Open source, local-only. Your data never leaves your machine. No accounts, no cloud, no 'anonymous' telemetry that isn't."

**Why Nomolo over alternatives:** Most privacy tools are defensive (block trackers, encrypt communications). Nomolo is offensive — it goes *into* the Conglomerates and brings your data *out*. Attack is the best defense. Especially when the attack involves insult fighting.

**Key content:** Architecture documentation. Security model. "Your vault is plain JSONL on your hard drive. Read it with grep. We dare you to find a more transparent format."

### Gamers / RPG Fans

**Who:** People who understand XP, character stats, progression systems, and the concept of grinding for loot.

**Hook:** "Your life is literally an RPG. Every email is loot. Every platform is a raid target. Level up by reclaiming your own data. The XP is real because the data is real."

**Why Nomolo over alternatives:** No other data tool has a progression system. No other data tool has insult fighting. No other data tool makes you feel like you're accomplishing something when you export your Gmail archive. Nomolo does. Because it is.

**Key content:** Character progression guide. Achievement list. Rank requirements. Villain profiles as raid boss guides.

### Developers

**Who:** Engineers who build things, contribute to open source, and appreciate well-structured projects.

**Hook:** "19 collectors, JSONL vaults, MCP integration, semantic search with ChromaDB. Fork it, extend it, make it yours. Write a collector for that weird service nobody else supports."

**Why Nomolo over alternatives:** Clean architecture. Modular collector system. Every vault is plain JSONL — no proprietary databases, no vendor lock-in at any layer. The codebase is a joy to extend. Also, the documentation is genuinely funny, which matters more than people admit.

**Key content:** Architecture diagram. "Adding a New Collector" guide. MCP integration docs. Contributing guidelines.

### Normal People

**Who:** Everyone who isn't in the above categories but has a Gmail account and ten years of photos in iCloud.

**Hook:** "See your entire digital life in one place. It's free and takes 2 minutes. You might discover things about yourself you didn't know."

**Why Nomolo over alternatives:** It's the only tool in this space that doesn't require a computer science degree to understand or use. The pirate theme makes it approachable. The web UI makes it visual. The "magic moment" — seeing your own data for the first time — is the hook.

**Key content:** 2-minute demo video. Use case stories ("I discovered I'd exchanged 4,000 messages with someone I lost touch with"). The identity snapshot feature.

### Content Creators

**Who:** Writers, artists, world-builders, and lore nerds who see the Flatcloud universe and want to play in it.

**Hook:** "The Flatcloud world is open. Write lore, create art, build on the universe. The Seven Seas need more cartographers."

**Why Nomolo over alternatives:** There is no alternative. No other data tool has a creative universe attached to it. The world-building is a unique asset that creates a community of contributors who aren't developers — artists, writers, and storytellers who expand the brand without writing a line of code.

**Key content:** THE_FLATCLOUD.md as source material. Character art templates. "Write your own Conglomerate" guide. Community art gallery.

## 9. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING

### The Landscape

The data sovereignty space has several players. None of them have users. Here's why.

| Competitor Type | Examples | Their Problem |
|----------------|----------|---------------|
| **Data export tools** | Google Takeout, various platform exporters | They exist! But they're built by the platforms themselves, which means they're designed to be just barely adequate enough to satisfy regulators while being maximally annoying to use. 48-hour wait times are not a technical limitation. They're a retention strategy. |
| **Privacy-focused platforms** | ProtonMail, Signal, etc. | They protect future data. They don't recover past data. Moving to Signal doesn't get your 10 years of WhatsApp messages back. |
| **Data management tools** | Various backup and archival tools | Technically competent. Visually barren. Emotionally dead. Using them feels like filing taxes, which is exactly why nobody uses them voluntarily. |
| **Personal knowledge management** | Obsidian, Notion, etc. | They organize data you *create*. They don't recover data that platforms *hold*. Different problem, different product. |

### Nomolo's Moat

**No one else in the data sovereignty space has personality.**

This is not a trivial observation. It's the entire strategy.

Competitors are serious, technical, and boring. Their landing pages feature stock photos of locks and shields. Their documentation reads like GDPR compliance audits. Their value proposition is "you should care about your data" — which, while true, has the persuasive power of "you should eat more vegetables."

Nomolo is the ONLY tool that makes data ownership FUN. The pirate world isn't a feature — it's a universe. It's not a theme bolted onto a utility. It's a narrative framework that makes every action meaningful, every milestone shareable, and every user a character in a story that's bigger than any individual product.

**The moat is cultural, not technical.** Anyone can build a Gmail collector. Nobody else is going to build the Seven Seas of Data, the Insult Data Fighting system, the Lobster Principle, and a twelve-villain rogues' gallery with Pratchett-grade footnotes. This is a creative moat. It gets deeper with every piece of lore, every character, every community contribution.

You can fork the code. You can't fork the culture.

## 10. MONETIZATION TEASER

### The SCUMM Bar is Free. The Grog Costs Extra.

The core product is free forever. This is non-negotiable. The growth engine depends on frictionless access to scanning, collecting, and the entire RPG layer. You never pay to reclaim your data. That would rather defeat the point.

### What's Free (Always)

| Feature | Why It's Free |
|---------|---------------|
| All scanning and data source discovery | This is the hook. It must be instant and free. |
| All data collection and import (19 collectors) | Reclaiming your data is a right, not a premium feature. |
| Local keyword search (FTS5) | Basic search over your own data should never cost money. |
| The entire RPG layer (ranks, stats, achievements, character cards) | The game IS the product. Gating it kills virality. |
| Dashboard, records browser, source management | The core UI is the free experience. |
| Insult Data Fighting, Lobster Meter, all gamification | Fun is free. That's the whole brand promise. |

### What Costs Extra (Future)

| Feature | Why It's Paid | Pricing Thinking |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| **AI Chat ("The One")** | RAG-powered conversations with your own data. Requires LLM inference (tokens cost money). | Freemium: small free token budget, Pro tier for unlimited. BYOK (bring your own API key) for power users. |
| **Smart Insights** | "You lost touch with 12 people." "Your spending on subscriptions doubled." Cross-source intelligence that requires AI processing. | Bundled with Pro tier. |
| **Marketplace** | Physical products generated from your data: life books, photo prints, timeline posters, data visualizations. Vendors create, Nomolo takes a provision. | Revenue share model. User pays vendor, Nomolo takes 15-20%. |
| **Premium Quests** | Extended narrative quests with deeper AI-powered insights. "The Lost Treasure of 2019" — an AI-guided journey through a specific year of your life. | Part of Pro tier or individual purchase. |

### Pricing Philosophy

The model mirrors the world-building: the SCUMM Bar is open to everyone. Walk in, sit down, compare loot, tell tales. Free. But if you want the *really good grog* — the AI that knows you, the insights that surprise you, the products that turn your data into something physical — that's where the doubloons come in.

The free tier should be so good that paying feels like a thank-you, not a toll. Pirates don't pay tolls. They pay for grog. There's a difference.[^2]

[^2]: The difference, for the record, is that grog is worth it. Tolls are not. Our entire business model is based on this distinction, which says something about our business model that we choose to interpret as charming.
